About Tweetstorm

Our Project

In recent times there has been a lot of discussion about the influence of the increased sharing of information and the platforms available to do this including how influential ‘fake news’ can be (Allcott, Gentzkow, 2017). Research on this topic has been conflicting including work that identified certain groups to only have a minor influence (Karlsen, Steen-Johnsen, Wollebæk, Enjolras, 2017), as well as others that found that social media can create network structures called polarized crowds, particularly around divisive topics (Zannettou, Caulfield, De Cristofaro, Sirivianos, Stringhini, Blackburn, 2019). While there have been these conflicting views the quantum of research that has found social media to be influential far outweighs those that don’t including research that found that sentiment of the public and the voting preferences of users can be identified by the volume of tweets (Tumasjan, Sprenger, Sandner, Welpe, 2010). Twitter, “a popular microblogging service where users create status messages (called “tweets”)” (Go, Bhayani, Huang, 2009) offers access to services that allows for the analysis of tweets and can be used to assess the platforms influence. There have been several pieces of research conducted and reviewing the methods currently used there is both value and shortcomings of each.
